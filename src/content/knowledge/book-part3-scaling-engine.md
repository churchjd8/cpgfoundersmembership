# Part III: Scaling the Engine
Source: "The Art of the Startup" by Jeff Church
Chapters: 13-20

## Key Stories & Anecdotes

### The Dog in the Car Window (Ch. 13)
Jeff compares the early days of scaling Suja to a dog with its head out a car window -- wide-eyed and holding on for dear life. The chaos of rapid growth was exhilarating and terrifying at once.

### Power Outage at 2:00 AM (Ch. 13)
One night the power went down at Suja's plant around 2:00 AM with too many orders to fill. The transformer was on the roof and only the landlord had access. Production workers climbed a tree next to the building and jumped from a branch onto the roof to reconnect the circuit without waking the landlord. Jeff celebrates this as the kind of scrappy, out-of-the-box thinking that turns lemons into lemonade.

### The Couple at Expo East (Ch. 14)
Jeff met a couple at Expo East who had started a juice company around the same time as Suja. They were careful, meticulous, and extremely risk-averse. Years later, they were stuck at a fraction of Suja's scale. Their desire for perfection kept them from seizing opportunities.

### Brian Sipe on Stress (Ch. 14)
During a memorable 1980s NFL season, the Cleveland Browns won 10 of their last 11 games with last-play victories. Quarterback Brian Sipe explained how he handled the pressure: "I can handle the stress pretty easily because all I have to do is look my two-year-old daughter in the eye and know that all of the stress that I'm dealing each week as an NFL quarterback, she will not only never know, but by the time she's a functioning adult in the world, I'll be long gone from the game of football." Jeff says this perspective resonated deeply and he leans on it often.

### Costco Test-and-Learn Rotations (Ch. 14)
Jeff developed a collaborative relationship with Costco buyers to test multiple innovation offers in 10-week rotations. Each failed rotation (marked with an X) and each successful one (marked with a star) provided real data. Instead of taking 3 extra months to perfect each offer, they kept the cadence and earned trust by being the first to tell the buyer when an offer needed replacement.

### Celery Juice Sprint with Trader Joe's (Ch. 14)
In 2019, Trader Joe's spotted the celery juice trend (boosted by The Rachael Ray Show). They called Suja. Trader Joe's typically preferred private label, but that process would have taken longer. From first call to product on shelves nationwide took less than 6 weeks -- a demonstration of speed and agility.

### "What's a Cat Man?" (Ch. 15)
After a grueling battle to gain doors and shelf space, Jeff told his head of sales Nicky: "I can't wait until we achieve full distribution and have nothing to worry about!" Nicky aggressively replied: "Two years from now, we'll be fighting tooth and nail to maintain our shelf space. We'll need the right cat man to win." Jeff asked, "What's a cat man?" -- triggering relentless teasing from the team. He had never needed a category manager at his previous companies (Nika Water, Lynx Grills) because they hadn't reached that scale.

### Dwight Richmond and Whole Foods' Cold-Pressed Bet (Ch. 15)
Dwight Richmond, formerly of Whole Foods and an early pioneer in natural foods, envisioned transforming the juice category beyond what Naked and Odwalla could achieve. He later explained that Whole Foods' decision to prioritize and expand the HPP cold-pressed juice category was driven by syndicated data trends, consumer insights, and forager feedback -- proving it wasn't just a fad and could handle multiple brands.

### Rowdy Energy Slotting Loss (Ch. 15)
At Rowdy Energy, Jeff paid nearly $1 million in one-time slotting fees to Albertsons. Because velocity was significantly below hurdle levels, Albertsons discontinued them after just 8 months.

### Jason Polinsky Promoted (Ch. 15)
Jeff promoted category manager Jason Polinsky to report directly to him after realizing how critical category management was strategically. Polinsky became one of the most valuable team members during Jeff's tenure at Suja.

### Board Member Challenges Innovation (Ch. 16)
A Suja board member said at a pre-board dinner with ~14 people: "Jeff, don't you see that what you think is disruptive innovation is just causing unnecessary churn?" Jeff responded: "We would prefer to have a single hero product like Red Bull or Coca-Cola instead of continuously innovating."

### 62 Cold-Pressed Juice Brands at Whole Foods (Ch. 16)
Six or seven years after Suja launched, a Whole Foods buyer told them she had 62 cold-pressed juice brands requesting meetings. She couldn't possibly see them all. This reinforced Jeff's belief: it's rarely ideal to be first to market, and it's dangerous to be last.

### Evolution Fresh and Blueprint Timing (Ch. 13)
Evolution Fresh and Blueprint Cleanse preceded Suja. Within 90 days of Suja's launch at Whole Foods in Southern California, Evolution was acquired by Starbucks and Blueprint was acquired by Hain Celestial. While they integrated, Suja stayed focused on scaling -- that timing gave them oxygen.

### Coca-Cola's Stages and Gates vs. Suja's Speed (Ch. 16)
When Coca-Cola invested in Suja, they introduced their "Stages and Gates" innovation framework -- a process that typically takes 16-18 months. Suja could take a new juice idea from concept to shelf in as little as 6 weeks.

### The Coca-Cola Investment (Ch. 17)
In July 2015, Coca-Cola valued Suja at $300 million (4.7x multiple on trailing twelve-month net sales). Coca-Cola and Goldman Sachs Private Equity together invested $150 million for a 50% stake. Coca-Cola invested $90 million; Goldman Sachs invested $60 million.

### Fighting for Angel Investor Fairness (Ch. 17)
The vast majority of Coca-Cola's $90M investment would have gone to ACG (private equity investor who had been in the deal less than 12 months) due to preference provisions. Jeff insisted on finding another investor. He secured Goldman Sachs' $60M, which allowed angel investors who had been with Suja for 3 years to sell roughly 40% of their holdings at an average 3.4x return while retaining 60% of their equity. Later, the angels got a second bite and their total return was roughly 10x.

### Quality Issue Mid-Deal (Ch. 17)
While in the final stages of the Coca-Cola deal, Jeff identified a potential quality issue and pressed pause. "There is no wrong time to do the right thing." This earned the respect of Coca-Cola's senior leadership.

### HPP Question That Nearly Killed the Deal (Ch. 17)
Jeff asked a leader in Coca-Cola's quality department whether HPP was a viable technology Coke would embrace. If the answer had gone the other way, the deal would have collapsed on the spot. "Years of work hung in the balance based on a single opinion."

### Mike Saint John as Internal Champion (Ch. 17)
Mike Saint John, then President of Minute Maid Juice, became Suja's internal champion at Coca-Cola. Without an internal champion willing to take reputational risk, Jeff says deals quietly die.

### Seth Goldman and John Foraker Advice (Ch. 17)
Seth Goldman (co-founder of Honest Tea) and John Foraker (former President of General Mills' natural sector) both gave Jeff the same advice about consumer backlash after a big corporate investment: "Don't argue with customers. Invite them to watch. Tell them to give you a year. Ask them to judge you by your actions, not by their assumptions."

### Challah Bread with Coca-Cola/Suja Logo (Ch. 18)
Linda and Jeff hosted Thanksgiving-style dinners at their home for key retailers. One memorable Target dinner featured a beautiful loaf of Challah bread designed with the Coca-Cola and Suja logos, created by Jeff's mother-in-law Rachel.

### Target Bed Photo (Ch. 18)
After a home-cooked meal and cocktails with the Target team visiting their manufacturing facility, someone suggested they all pile into an oversized bed for a photo. Jeff notes that real trust is built through shared meals, stories, and genuine laughter -- not Zoom calls and formal meetings.

### Walmart Smoothie Trade (Ch. 18)
Walmart wanted Suja to launch a Walmart-exclusive plant-based smoothie. The production team hated the idea. But Jeff agreed to produce a limited, operationally inefficient run at unattractive economics in exchange for expanding distribution of higher-performing SKUs. The calculated trade paid off -- Walmart remains one of Suja's largest customers.

### Target Buyer's Barcode Advice (Ch. 18)
Before launching in Walmart, a senior Target buyer advised Jeff not to use identical barcodes or pack sizes across all retailers. If they did, Target would price-match Walmart's EDLP. Suja launched a 10.5-ounce bottle at Walmart and a 12-ounce bottle at Target to preserve pricing integrity.

### Bill Weiland and Presence Marketing (Ch. 18)
Bill Weiland and Presence Marketing were essential to Suja's early Whole Foods launch. Bill was selective about brands he represented. At one Christmas party, after a few cocktails, Bill somehow left with Jeff's Nutcracker decoration.

### Suja Sales Team Members (Ch. 18)
- Nicky's sales team proactively brought solutions for underperforming SKUs
- Lauren Soward managed Target and Walmart while absorbing Trader Joe's after Jessa's departure, without adding headcount
- Jody navigated complex growth with Kroger and Publix during difficult internal seasons
- Gretchen reinvigorated Whole Foods and helped launch Elevated Nutrients

### Coca-Cola's Call on July 3, 2018 (Ch. 19)
On July 3, 2018, with his house full of family preparing for the Fourth of July, Scott Uzell called to inform Jeff that Coke would not be moving forward with acquiring the remaining portion of Suja. Six weeks earlier, Coke's management and HR teams had visited to discuss integration. Jeff walked downstairs after the call and wept in front of his boys.

### The Next Morning in Mayville (Ch. 19)
Jeff woke up on July 4th in Mayville, New York, to the sound of fireworks and the annual parade. Blue skies, 82 degrees, not a cloud in sight. He felt sick to his stomach and wondered for a split second if he'd dreamed it.

### Operating with Less Than $100K in the Bank (Ch. 19)
After Coke's rejection, Todd Fisher (CFO) and Jeff lived inside weekly cash projections. At times they were operating with less than $100,000 in the bank on a $100M revenue company.

### Cutting Marketing, Revenue Grew (Ch. 19)
After cutting marketing by more than $4 million, revenue grew by about 10% in 2019. This taught Jeff that strong brands with loyal customers don't always need massive spending to grow.

### Fortune Teller Predicts Wellness Shots (Ch. 20)
In 2014, Jeff's wife Linda visited a fortune teller named Tara, who predicted that the product creating the most value for Suja would be wellness shots. At the time, Bryan had just begun experimenting with early formulations. Years later, it proved true.

### Costco Head-to-Head Shot Test (Ch. 20)
A Costco buyer proposed a head-to-head test between Suja's wellness shot and a competitor's product. He also wanted Suja to develop a plant-based milk (which they had no experience producing). The plant milk struggled, but the wellness shots took off -- selling about $2,000 per week per club.

### Kombucha vs. Wellness Shots (Ch. 20)
Suja's kombucha was technically strong (they'd solved the alcohol level consistency challenge), but consumers didn't see Suja as a kombucha brand. Kombucha was outsourced at roughly 12% gross margin. Wellness shots, produced internally, generated margins closer to 60%. Swapping kombucha for shots moved company-wide gross margin from ~32% to ~40%.

## Frameworks & Models

### Embrace Chaos Framework (Ch. 13)
Chaos signifies your brand is pushing boundaries. Chaos offers opportunity disguised as inefficiency. Without chaos, there may not be opportunities for innovation and market disruption.

### Make vs. Buy Decision (Ch. 13)
Most CPG brands use third-party co-manufacturers to avoid heavy capital investment. Suja chose vertical integration. While initially cash-intensive, once capacity is filled, you generate strong operating leverage by eliminating an entire layer of cost.

### Market Entry Timing Sweet Spot (Ch. 13)
3rd, 4th, or 5th entrant in a category is the sweet spot. You benefit from pioneers who proved the concept. Once past the 10th entrant, breaking through becomes dramatically harder. "Being early enough to matter and disciplined enough to execute is often better than being first."

### 80/20 Rule Applied to Startups (Ch. 14)
80% of results come from 20% of efforts. Getting a product 80% ready into market for consumer feedback is better than perfecting it. "A good product that evolves based on customer feedback can often outperform a 'perfect' product that is rigid and unresponsive."

### Hero SKU Strategy (Ch. 16)
The long-term goal for any repeat-purchase product is to identify one or more SKUs that create leverage across the entire business. A hero single-serve SKU often generates an additional 100%+ in revenue through secondary formats. If you don't have at least one SKU outperforming the rest by 30%+, you don't have a hero SKU.

### SWOT Analysis Applied (Ch. 16)
Suja's key strength was manufacturing. While owning manufacturing is a boat anchor when underutilized, it becomes a strategic asset as you grow: landing new retailers, earning more shelf space, and innovating faster than competitors.

### Plan B Strategy (Ch. 16)
When launching a new product line, always develop an alternative in parallel. At least half the time, Plan A doesn't materialize. Plan B offers paths to preserving shelf space, maintaining retailer confidence, and buying time for awareness and trial.

### Velocity Benchmarking Against Category Median (Ch. 15)
- 80%+ of median: You reign supreme, can expand off-cycle
- 60%-80%: Likely able to increase SKU count in next reset
- 50%: Doing okay
- 30%-50%: Need a solid plan or you won't make the next reset
- Below 20%: Something is seriously amiss

### Two-Step Deal Structure (Ch. 17)
Advantages: Easier to close, provides partial liquidity, founders maintain operational autonomy. Disadvantages: Unable to fully integrate teams/processes, unclear decision-making authority, second step may never happen if not contractually required.

### Core Principles of Retail Collaboration (Ch. 18)
1. Help the retailer win the category
2. Understand their model better than your competitors do
3. Play the long game
4. Protect the ecosystem
5. Reinforce your story relentlessly
6. Balance energy with data
7. Offer smart exclusives
8. Stay flexible

### Distribution Sequencing Strategy (Ch. 18)
Build strong presence at Whole Foods, Target, Costco, and Kroger before entering Walmart. Walmart's EDLP model creates pricing tension across channels. Enter Walmart in the back half of distribution build-out once brand equity and pricing power are established.

### From Disruption to Discipline (Ch. 19)
After Coke's rejection, the way forward stopped being about disruption and became about discipline: cut marketing $4M+, eliminate low-margin outsourced products, reduce inefficient manual assemblies, shift toward higher-margin shots, launch $4M raw-material cost program, reduce operating expenses $3M+.

### Expansion Should Build on Core Identity (Ch. 20)
Expansion strengthens a brand when it builds on what you already do well. Random expansion weakens it. Suja's kombucha failed because consumers didn't see Suja as a kombucha brand. Wellness shots succeeded because they were a concentrated extension of cold-pressed juice.

## Tactical Advice

### Scaling Operations
- Relentless focus on lowering COGS and expanding gross margin delivers one of the highest returns in business
- Studying and redesigning your supply chain is one of the highest-impact exercises you can do
- If you do your own manufacturing, develop innovation as a core competency -- you can innovate faster than co-manufacturers
- Shelf life dictates everything: route to market, inventory risk, distribution reach, and consumer access

### Using Data to Win
- Increasing velocity (how fast product sells per store) matters far more than adding doors
- Key syndicated data metrics to master: ACV, Dollars per Point of Distribution, USW, % Sold on Promo
- Be proactive with buyers -- raise underperforming SKU issues yourself rather than waiting
- When buyers have ideas that feel "out there," try it their way; if it fails, revisit your solution
- 1,000 low-traffic Piggly Wiggly stores have much lower ACV than 300 Walmart stores

### Innovation Strategy
- Use short-term exclusives (3-6 months, sometimes by channel) to secure distribution
- Invite retailers into the innovation process to increase enthusiasm and internal advocacy
- If fewer than half your SKUs consistently meet retailer velocity requirements, keep refining portfolio
- Innovation is not churn if it improves turns and strengthens economics

### Strategic Partnerships
- No deal survives without an internal champion on the acquiring side willing to take reputational risk
- For consumer backlash after corporate investment: don't argue, invite customers to watch, ask for a year to be judged by actions
- Two-step deals create complexity: being half in and half out creates friction founders must anticipate

### Retail Relationships
- Help the retailer win the category, not just push your product
- Buyers are measured on category performance, not your brand's survival
- Use different pack sizes/barcodes across retailers to preserve pricing integrity
- A Costco 13-week rotation is a gift -- test innovation without committing to full-year reset
- Whole Foods is also a marketing platform, not just a P&L line
- Actively seek out retailer programs (like Target's Made to Matter) for visibility and internal advocacy

### Financial Discipline
- Adopt bottom-up budgeting instead of aspirational top-down forecasting
- When forced to remove everything non-essential, you discover what truly drives performance
- Strong brands with loyal customers don't always need massive spending to grow
- Communicate benefit outcomes to consumers, not ingredient lists
- Product mix shifts can have more impact than cost-cutting
