# Part IV: The Exit & Personal Reflections
Source: "The Art of the Startup" by Jeff Church
Chapters: 21-22

## Key Stories & Anecdotes

### The Quiet Search for a Buyer (Ch. 21)
By early 2019, after stabilizing the company, Jeff hosted management presentations with more than 15 potential buyers. None made an offer. "That didn't surprise me. We were still a work in progress. Sometimes when you're doing the right things, you just have to keep going."

### The Turnaround in Numbers (Ch. 21)
- 2018: Lost about $10 million on an EBITDA basis
- 2019: Generated positive $3 million EBITDA
- Gross margins improved materially; revenue streams became more diversified
- Jeff describes being "left at the altar" as the force that created honesty and discipline

### Stepping Down as CEO (Ch. 21)
By May 2019, Jeff had brought the company back to profitability for the first time in company history. But he was exhausted -- 8 years of 80-hour weeks had taken their toll. He knew the company needed a different leader for its next phase. His instincts were shifting back toward growth while the board's priorities were stability and debt reduction. Bob DeBrode joined in August 2019 as the new leader. Jeff officially left in January 2020.

### The Sale to Paine Schwartz (Ch. 21)
In 2020, Suja was sold to Paine Schwartz for approximately $300 million. Today the company generates more than $300 million in revenue with roughly 20% EBITDA margins.

### Coca-Cola's Rejection as a Gift (Ch. 21)
"Coca-Cola's decision felt devastating at the time. In hindsight, it was a gift. It forced discipline. It forced rigor. It forced maturity."

### Jeff's Kids' Failed Startups (Ch. 22)
Two of Jeff's kids started their own consumer businesses. Both ultimately failed and had to close. As a parent used to being in charge, it was incredibly painful to watch. But Jeff told both: "You've been given a gift you'll have for the rest of your life." Both internalized that and went on to do great things. Jeff says the skills gained are worth more than the most expensive MBA on the planet.

### "Suja Years" (Ch. 22)
At Suja, the team gained about 7 years of traditional business experience in a single year. They called them "Suja years."

### Bill Weiland's Employee Investment Program (Ch. 22)
During a fundraising round, Bill Weiland (Presence Marketing) created an incentive program where his employees could invest a portion of their retirement allocations in Suja. After the company sold, a mid-level Presence employee came up to Jeff at a trade show and thanked him profusely -- the money she made on her Suja investment put both of her kids through college.

### 350 Employees, 1,000+ Family Members (Ch. 22)
At the height of growth during Jeff's tenure, Suja had nearly 350 employees, meaning well over 1,000 family members were supported by those jobs.

### The Two Brothers Who Invested (Ch. 22)
Jeff is friends with two brothers who both invested in his ventures over the years. One has only invested in Jeff's winning companies; the other has only invested in the losing ones.

### Drinking and Rehab (Ch. 22)
While running a "better-for-you" beverage company, Jeff neglected his own health. After working 80-hour weeks for nearly 8 years, he was completely burned out. He was drinking far too much alcohol. After leaving the company, he spent a month in rehab to reset his life and health. His wife Linda and family told him they believed that if he hadn't stepped away from Suja when he did, he might not still be here today.

### Rowdy Energy Final Investment (Ch. 22)
When Jeff's energy drink company (Rowdy Energy) was failing, he invested several additional chunks of cash trying to keep it alive. Before he knew it, he had invested nearly $1 million in the company's final year. "Optimism is part of being an entrepreneur, but I should have been more disciplined."

### Parents' Marriage (Ch. 22)
Jeff's parents ended a 25-year marriage in part because his father wanted to pursue a risky venture later in life while his mother wanted stability. Jeff's father traveled constantly for work, and Jeff ended up much closer to his mom as a result. He sometimes wonders whether his own kids will feel the same distance from him.

### 3:00 AM Wake-Ups (Ch. 22)
"There are nights when you wake up at 3:00 a.m., staring into the dark, worried about payroll, investors, or whether the company will survive."

## Frameworks & Models

### The Good, The Bad, and The Ugly Framework (Ch. 22)
Jeff structures his personal reflections in three categories:

**The Good:**
- Rush of seeing your product on shelves for the first time
- Daily evolution -- no two weeks are the same
- Being your own Michelangelo, painting the culture of your company
- Relationships forged in wartime go deeper and last for life
- Network effect: "one degree of separation from almost anyone in the CPG world"
- Financial upside: can leap multiple socioeconomic levels in a single career
- Creating jobs that support families

**The Bad:**
- ~50% of time over 30 years spent raising money
- Of 30 years building startups, only ~5 years actually making money
- Retail is unforgiving: constant rejection, slow approval cycles, costly slotting fees
- Operational surprises: cost increases, supply chain disruptions, packaging errors, recalls
- Poor hiring decisions that set you back months
- Financial stress as a constant companion

**The Ugly:**
- Relationship costs: founders who became consumed ended up divorced
- Enormous pressure on entrepreneur's spouse (who shares risks with less control)
- Every new venture is essentially a bet on portions of joint retirement
- Missed dinners, cut vacations short, absent from parent-teacher meetings
- Physical toll: gray hair, exhaustion, weight fluctuations, stress-related health problems
- Identity tied to startup -- if it fails, sense of self unravels; if it succeeds, ego inflation
- Burnout is real

### Resilience Through Forced Discipline (Ch. 21)
Sometimes leadership doesn't look heroic. Sometimes it looks like spreadsheets, uncomfortable cuts, hard conversations, and patience. Being "left at the altar" forces honesty, confronting weaknesses you've been rationalizing, and alignment between capital, operations, and strategy.

## Tactical Advice

### On Exits
- More than 15 potential buyers saw presentations before the eventual sale -- persistence matters
- Sometimes you have to keep going when doing the right things even if results aren't immediate
- Diversification of revenue streams creates resilience and makes the business more attractive
- Getting "left at the altar" can be the catalyst for the discipline that makes a future exit possible
- Founders must recognize when the company needs a different leader for its next phase

### On the Founder's Personal Journey
- After every successful exit, set aside at least 10% of after-tax proceeds in a retirement account you cannot touch
- Startup careers rarely include traditional retirement programs
- Communicate openly with your spouse about risks, expectations, and possible outcomes
- Exercise, sleep, nutrition, and regular health checkups should not be optional
- Build support systems: mentors, coaches, hobbies outside the business, personal boundaries, trusted peers
- Don't tie your entire identity to your startup
- The education gained in a startup in a short period is one of the most valuable aspects of the journey
- Human nature tends to forget the bad and remember the good -- most founders would do it all again

### On Leadership Transitions
- Know when your instincts (growth) are misaligned with what the company needs (stability)
- Stepping down can be the most important leadership decision you make
- Leaving is painful, but transitions are part of building something that lasts
- Bring the company to profitability before stepping away -- never leave without that milestone

### On Financial Discipline
- Stop throwing good money after bad -- Jeff invested nearly $1M in Rowdy Energy's final year out of optimism
- Of 30 years of building startups, only about 5 were spent actually making money -- this is normal but worth understanding going in
- The pressure on relationships intensifies when every new venture is a bet on joint retirement savings
