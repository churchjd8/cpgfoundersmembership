Ryan put up the slide and I thought the numbers were wrong.

We're sitting in a session I put together for our founder group -- maybe fifteen CPG founders, early stage and mid-stage, all trying to figure out digital. Ryan runs Amazon strategy for a beverage brand I co-invest in. Three years on the platform, doing around $500,000 a month. And he pulls up a cohort analysis that shows one number I keep coming back to.

73% of total revenue comes from repeat customers.

Not 30%. Not 50%. Seventy-three percent. That's $4.3 million out of roughly $6 million in annual sales from people who have bought before. First-time buyers are generating the other 27% -- they're important, but they're not the business. The business is the person who bought in March and came back in April and came back again in May.

Here's the twist. On that very first sale, they lose money. The unit economics don't go positive until the second or third purchase. By the time someone has bought four or more times, they're worth $58 in cumulative profit above what it cost to acquire them. The lifetime value of a 4x-plus customer is $293.

That's the DTC flywheel. And most CPG founders I work with don't have a mental model for it at all.

---

## You Will Lose Money on the First Sale. Plan for It.

This is the hardest mental shift for a founder coming from a retail-first background. In retail, you're thinking about velocity -- units per store per week. Every unit should be margin-positive from day one. The sell-through has to work right now.

DTC is different. The first sale is an investment in a customer relationship, not a one-time transaction. You spend roughly $25-30 to acquire a new customer through Amazon ads or Meta. You ship the product. First-order margin is thin. The math looks ugly on a spreadsheet. But that customer -- if your product is genuinely worth coming back to -- returns in about 23 days. The median days between first and second purchase on that beverage brand? 23 days.

And the second purchase costs almost nothing to earn. Neither does the third, or the fourth.

CPG is a "penny profit" business. The pennies matter. Which means losing a few pennies on sale number one to earn $293 over a lifetime isn't charity -- it's the model.

The question the data is really asking you is simple: is your product good enough to earn that repeat?

That's the gate. All the conversion optimization in the world won't save a product people don't want to buy again. If you've been running for three months and you're not seeing at least a 15% repeat rate... stop spending more on acquisition. It's a product problem. Price, flavor, format, formula -- something isn't connecting. Hope is not a strategy, and neither is running more ads at a broken product.

## The Mix Pack Rule Nobody Follows Until They Run Out

Here's something I've told every founder I've worked with, and most of them don't act on it until they prove it to themselves the hard way.

On Amazon, 70% of your inventory allocation should be mix packs. Minimum. On some brands, it runs 80-85%. Subscribe and Save numbers on mix packs far outpace single flavors -- and once a customer is on auto-ship, you've basically won.

I've been saying this for years. And every single time, founders under-allocate. They think consumers want to commit to a single flavor. They think the mix pack is a compromise product. They're wrong. Consistently wrong. And then they scramble when the mix pack runs out and the single flavors sit.

One of the operators I work with put it plainly: "Every time I talk to a founder, I encourage them to go to that 70% level on mix packs, and they don't. And then they're short on the mix packs. And then they're scrambling."

Three years. $500K a month. The mix pack percentage hasn't changed. That pattern doesn't go away with scale.

## Your Website Is a Bucket Full of Holes

Austin -- who runs a digital performance agency I bring in to work with our founder group -- described Shopify optimization the best way I've heard: "Your website, your email, your SMS is like a bucket full of holes. You're just pouring water through it, and you're just trying to plug the holes as fast as possible."

94-95% of visitors to your Shopify store don't buy on their first visit. That's not a bad brand. That's e-commerce. The question is what happens to those people after they leave.

The brands that win build retention infrastructure before they pour fuel into acquisition. The browse abandonment email that fires when someone leaves a product page. The abandoned cart sequence. The welcome series that spends two weeks educating a new subscriber on why your product works. The SMS flow that converts at a higher rate per subscriber than email because people actually read their texts.

Remarketing via email and SMS costs $1-2 per customer. Paid ad remarketing costs $5-10. You do the math.

One of the case studies Austin walked our group through: a Korean seaweed soup brand that went from $5,000 a month to $400,000 a month in less than eight months. Not by running massive ad campaigns. By optimizing conversion first. Their site converts at 6%. Industry average is 2-3%. They discovered through customer feedback that 20% of their bounced visitors were vegan -- so they built a vegan option and watched conversion lift. Then they built audience-specific landing pages for moms, wellness buyers, and Korean/Asian-inspired households, each with matched email flows and pop-ups.

Beautiful websites that convert at 1% are one of the most expensive mistakes I see. "I've seen people spend $30-40K on a website that looks beautiful when you look at it, and then you get to the conversion rate, it's like 1%, and you're like, well, this isn't helping you." Get the conversion architecture right before you spend a dollar on traffic.

## What DTC Tells You That Retail Can't

Here's the part most founders completely miss. The real value of a DTC channel isn't the revenue -- it's the signal.

In retail, you spend $20,000 a month or more on Nielsen or SPINS data to understand what's happening on shelf. It's lagged. It's aggregated. You can't see individual purchase behavior. You can't identify which customers are loyal versus one-and-done.

On Amazon and Shopify, that data is available in real time. Free. Cohort analysis from week one. You can see repeat rate, average days between purchases, lifetime value by acquisition source, which SKUs drive loyalty versus just trial.

This is the thing about "don't confuse distribution gains with velocity gains." The same principle applies in DTC: don't confuse traffic gains with retention gains. Getting people to your site is table stakes. Getting them to come back is the actual business.

And here's something that surprised me when Ryan first showed it: branded search volume on Amazon rises when you run off-platform campaigns -- Meta ads, out-of-home, events. You can't perfectly attribute it. But when one of our founders (Ali, who came from the Glossier world) ran regional out-of-home or opened pop-ups, she could see pops in those geos across Amazon and direct channels roughly 90-120 days later. The halo effect is real. DTC lets you measure it in a way retail never could.

## Where to Start if You're Building This Now

A few practical things for founders who are early in their DTC build:

**Start with Shopify.** Not another platform. It's so far ahead in e-commerce infrastructure and AI-enabled tools that the choice isn't close.

**Set your minimum ad budget.** $5,000-10,000 a month on Meta, similar on Amazon. $10 a day is technically usable, but "you can learn with $10 a day -- it's just going to take you years." The amount you spend is the timeline in which you learn your audience.

**Optimize conversion before you run ads.** Get your email flows, SMS sequences, and landing pages in place first. Running paid traffic to a leaky site is just burning money faster.

**Track cohorts, not just totals.** Watch monthly cohorts and their repeat rates over time. January cohorts underperform -- "New Year, New Me" buyers who try a health product once and disappear. July cohorts underperform too -- Prime Day deal-hunters. Know the seasonality of your customer quality, not just your revenue.

**Target 3x CAC to LTV.** If you're spending $25 to acquire a customer, you need at least $75 in 12-month lifetime value from them. If that math doesn't work, fix the product or fix the price before you scale.

He still survived. He still got into Sprouts. And now he's going to launch on Amazon and start performance marketing from a position of product-market fit instead of chasing it.

His advice to the group was better than anything I could add: "Don't do what we did. Do it sooner."

You can market your way into trial. You cannot market your way into loyalty. The first sale proves your marketing. The fourth sale proves your product.

Build for the fourth sale.
