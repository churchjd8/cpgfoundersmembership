# Trade Spend Optimization: How to Stop Bleeding Margin at Retail

Most CPG founders obsess over gross margin. They negotiate hard with their co-man, they find cheaper packaging, they shave ingredient costs. And then they sign their first major retail agreement and watch 20% of their revenue disappear into trade spend - and wonder what happened.

Trade spend is the single biggest margin killer most early-stage CPG brands never see coming. I've watched founders hit $10M in retail velocity only to discover they're operating at negative contribution margin because their trade programs are completely out of control.

Here's the truth: trade spend is not optional. But getting destroyed by it is.

## What Trade Spend Actually Is

Trade spend is everything you pay to retail partners to get and keep your product on shelf and moving. It breaks down into a few major buckets:

**Slotting fees** - The upfront cost to get on shelf. In conventional grocery, expect $5,000-$15,000 per SKU per region, sometimes more. Whole Foods and natural specialty have historically been lower or waived for emerging brands, but that's changing.

**TPRs (Temporary Price Reductions)** - When the retailer runs your product on sale, you fund part or all of the discount. If your $8 product goes on sale at $6 and you're funding $1.50 of that, you're giving away margin every time someone buys on deal.

**MCBs (Manufacturer Charge Backs)** - Deductions the retailer takes against your invoice. These come in the form of damaged goods, short shipments, compliance issues, and a dozen other line items. New founders see these and think it's a mistake. It's not. Budget for 2-5% of gross revenue in MCBs from day one in conventional.

**Display and feature fees** - Want an end cap? Want to be in the weekly circular? That costs money. Some programs are worth it. Many are not.

**Off-invoice allowances** - A blanket percentage off every invoice during a promotional period. This one is particularly dangerous because it hits every unit sold, not just the promoted ones.

At scale, I've seen trade spend run anywhere from 15% to 28% of gross revenue depending on the channel mix. Natural specialty tends to be lighter - call it 8-14%. Conventional grocery is where it gets brutal.

## The Benchmark You Need to Know

Here's the number I give every founder I work with: **your trade spend should never exceed your gross margin buffer.**

If you're running 45% gross margin, you theoretically have room for trade spend before you go negative on contribution. But by the time you factor in freight, warehousing, broker fees, and overhead, your actual ceiling is much lower - probably 18-22% of revenue max in conventional grocery.

If you're at 38% gross margin trying to compete in conventional grocery with 20% trade spend, you are building a burning house. I've seen it happen. I've watched founders raise another $3M to fund operations only to realize the operations themselves are structurally unprofitable because they never controlled trade.

## The Four Numbers You Need to Track

Most brands I talk to can't answer these four questions. If you can't, stop and fix this before you add another retailer.

**1. Trade spend as a percentage of net revenue, by customer.** Not blended. By customer. Your Kroger trade rate and your Sprouts trade rate should be tracked separately because they're completely different programs.

**2. Promoted velocity vs. non-promoted velocity.** If your product only sells when it's on deal, you have a brand problem, not a trade problem. The goal of a well-run promotion is to drive trial that converts to full-price repeat purchases. If your promoted velocity is 5x your non-promoted velocity, buyers love you on deal. That's not a business - that's a coupon.

**3. Trade spend ROI per program.** Every TPR, every feature, every display - calculate the incremental units sold and the margin generated against what the program cost. If a feature ad costs $8,000 and drives $3,000 in incremental margin, that program is destroying value. Run it only if you have a strategic reason to maintain the relationship.

**4. Your effective net margin after trade.** This is the number that tells you whether your retail business is actually making money. Gross margin minus trade spend minus freight minus warehousing minus broker fees. If this is negative for a major customer and has been for more than two quarters, you have a hard conversation to have.

## How to Actually Optimize It

At Suja, we were meticulous about trade spend as we scaled from regional to national distribution. Here's what actually moves the needle:

**Negotiate program structures upfront, not retroactively.** Buyers will push for open-ended allowance programs. Push back. Get specific: "We'll commit to two TPR windows per quarter at $X per unit funded." Vague commitments always become expensive commitments.

**Treat slotting fees as customer acquisition cost.** If it costs you $40,000 to get into a 200-store Kroger region and your average unit contribution after trade is $1.20, you need to sell 33,000 units just to recoup slotting. Does that math work given your velocity projections? Run it. Most founders never do.

**Build post-event analysis into your calendar.** Every major promotional event - two weeks after it ends, you should be pulling the scan data and analyzing lift. Was the promoted velocity 1.5x baseline? 2x? Did it come back down after the promotion ended? This data is how you negotiate better terms next year and how you decide which programs to kill.

**Audit your deductions monthly.** MCBs and deductions will pile up fast if you're not watching them. Some are legitimate. Many are not. I've seen brands recover six figures by simply disputing deductions they should never have accepted. Most distributors and retailers have a dispute window - if you miss it, the money is gone.

**Use your broker as a shield.** Your broker should be fighting these battles for you. If your broker isn't actively managing deductions, negotiating program terms, and providing post-event analysis, you either have the wrong broker or you haven't given them the right incentives. Brokers who get paid only on commission have no structural reason to protect your margins - they just want velocity. Build trade performance into your broker scorecard.

## When to Walk Away from a Retail Partner

This is the conversation most founders are terrified to have. But sometimes the right move is to exit a retailer.

If a customer represents 30% of your volume but is running at negative contribution margin even after 12 months of trying to optimize, that customer is subsidized by your other profitable business. Every dollar of working capital you tie up in their inventory is a dollar you can't deploy in a channel that actually makes money.

I'm not saying exit lightly. Retail presence has brand value beyond direct margin. But I've watched founders stay too long with structurally bad retail relationships because they're afraid of what pulling the product says about their brand. What actually kills your brand is running out of money.

## The Practical Starting Point

If you've never built a clean trade spend model, start here this week:

Pull your last 12 months of invoices from every retailer. Add up every dollar you paid out in slotting, TPR funding, MCBs, off-invoice allowances, and display fees. Divide that total by your gross revenue from those same accounts. That's your blended trade rate.

If it's above 18% and you're in natural specialty, you have a problem. If it's above 22% in conventional grocery, you have a serious problem. If you don't know what it is, you have the most serious problem of all.

Trade spend is not glamorous. Nobody posts about it on LinkedIn. But it is the difference between a brand that makes it to a profitable exit and a brand that raises three rounds, hits $15M in retail velocity, and still can't find a buyer because the unit economics never worked.

Control your trade spend or it will control your future.
