Strategy gets the headlines. Execution pays the bills. After scaling Suja to $100M in five years and building 7 other companies, here are the execution, talent, and financial lessons that actually made the difference.

## Execution: How You Win in the Trenches

### Reward Scrappy, Out-of-the-Box Actions

The best moments at Suja weren't in boardrooms. They were when a team member figured out how to get us into a retailer through a back door nobody thought of. When someone drove product samples to a buyer's house on a Sunday. When an intern created a social post that went viral.

Foster a culture that celebrates these moments. Reward the hustle. The big CPG companies can't out-scrappy you, and that's your biggest edge.

### If Your Key Differentiator Gets Blocked, You're in Trouble

At Suja, our differentiator was High Pressure Processing (HPP) - it let us make a raw, cold-pressed juice with real shelf life. If a competitor had somehow blocked our access to HPP technology, our entire business model would have collapsed.

Know what your key differentiator is and protect it aggressively. If it's a proprietary process, patent it. If it's a supplier relationship, lock it up with a contract. If it's a talent advantage, pay to keep those people. If someone can take your differentiator away, your $100M path is compromised.

### Balance Brand Authenticity with Distribution Speed

There's a constant tension between building brand authenticity and expanding distribution fast enough to outpace competition. Go too slow on distribution and competitors fill the white space. Go too fast and you outrun your brand awareness, leading to poor velocity.

At Suja, we found the sweet spot by building brand love in key markets first, then expanding with confidence that consumer pull would follow. It's not a formula - it's a judgment call you have to make market by market.

### Don't Let "Great" Get in the Way of "Good"

Perfectionism kills speed. I've seen founders delay a launch by six months because the label wasn't perfect. I've seen them miss a retail window because they wanted one more round of consumer testing. Good enough, shipped on time, beats perfect, shipped late - every single time.

Move fast. Iterate. The market will tell you what needs to be better. Your conference room won't.

### Watch for Fatal Flaws Lurking Below the Surface

The obvious mistakes are easy to avoid. It's the ones hiding just below the surface that get you. A co-manufacturer who's financially unstable. A key employee who's quietly interviewing. A retailer who's about to reset your category. Stay paranoid. Ask hard questions. Dig deeper than the surface.

## Talent: Your Team Is Your Company

### Set Clear Expectations and Track Performance

This applies to your best people and your weakest. High performers need to know what "great" looks like so they can keep pushing. Underperformers need to know exactly where they're falling short so they can improve or move on.

Vague expectations produce vague results. Set specific, measurable goals. Review them monthly. Don't wait for annual reviews to have hard conversations.

### Mediocre People in Key Roles Create Toxicity

One mediocre VP will do more damage than ten bad interns. When someone in a position of influence isn't performing, everyone sees it. Your best people get frustrated. Your culture erodes. Performance standards drop across the board.

Move fast on upgrading key roles. It's uncomfortable, but every week you wait makes the problem worse.

### Create an Ownership Mentality

When employees think like owners, everything changes. They watch costs. They fight for the brand. They solve problems instead of escalating them. At Suja, the team members who had equity - even small amounts - behaved fundamentally differently than those who didn't.

Equity, profit-sharing, transparent financials, decision-making authority - these all drive ownership mentality. Find what works for your stage and culture, and lean into it.

### Value Diversity but Demand Open-Mindedness

Diverse teams make better decisions. Period. But diversity without open-mindedness and transparency is just a collection of people who disagree. Create an environment where different perspectives are welcomed and debated productively, not just tolerated.

### Big CPG People in Startup Roles - Handle with Care

Hiring from Pepsi, Nestle, or Procter & Gamble can bring incredible expertise. But the transition from big CPG to an emerging, chaotic brand is brutal. Many don't make it. They're used to budgets, support teams, and established processes that don't exist in a startup.

When hiring big CPG talent, screen specifically for adaptability, comfort with ambiguity, and willingness to do things themselves instead of delegating to teams that don't exist yet.

## Financial: The Math That Keeps You Alive

### Target 50%+ Gross Margins

This is the single most important financial target for an emerging CPG brand. At 50%+ gross margins, you have room for trade spend, marketing investment, and capital expenditures without being entirely dependent on fundraising. Below 40%, you're in a constant cash crunch.

If your margins are below 50%, fix them before you scale. Raising prices is hard, but it's easier at 200 doors than at 2,000.

### Budget with Bottom-Up Revenue Forecasting

Top-down forecasting - "the market is $5B, we'll capture 1%" - is fiction. Bottom-up forecasting - "we're in 500 doors at $150/store/week, expanding to 800 doors next quarter" - is reality.

Build your budget from the store level up. It forces honesty about growth assumptions and gives you credible numbers for investor conversations.

### You Can Force Profitability

At a certain scale, profitability is a choice. You can cut marketing, reduce headcount, and slow expansion to show EBITDA. This is a powerful tool - it proves to investors that your business model works, even if you choose to reinvest for growth.

Know your "profitability lever" at all times. Being able to say "we could be profitable next quarter if we wanted to" is the most powerful sentence in a fundraising pitch.

### Start Small, Stay Lean, Go

Keep your burn rate down in the early days. Every dollar you save before product-market fit is a dollar that extends your runway. I've seen founders raise $2M and burn through it in 8 months on office space, salaries, and marketing before they had velocity to show for it.

Start small. Prove the model. Then invest aggressively.

### CPG Is a Penny-Profit Business - The Pennies Matter

In CPG, you might make $0.50 per unit on a good day. That means every penny of cost matters. A $0.02 increase in packaging cost across a million units is $20,000. A 1% improvement in fill rate saves tens of thousands annually.

Build a culture that sweats the pennies. Review your COGS line by line. Negotiate every input cost. The brands that win in CPG are the ones that are obsessive about margin at the unit level.
