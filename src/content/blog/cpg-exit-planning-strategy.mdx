Most CPG founders think about exits the wrong way. They build a brand, it starts doing well, and then they wait for someone to knock on their door. Sometimes that works. More often, you leave tens of millions of dollars on the table because you weren't positioned correctly, you didn't know who the right buyers were, or you negotiated from weakness instead of strength.

I've been on both sides of this. Eight companies, $700M+ in exits. Suja Juice - sold a majority stake to Coca-Cola. Other exits across beverage and food to strategic and financial buyers. I've made the mistakes. I've also done it right. Here's what actually matters.

## Start Thinking About Your Exit on Day One

This isn't morbid. It's strategic. The decisions you make in year one shape your optionality in year five.

Every major decision - your cap table structure, your category positioning, your retail distribution footprint, your gross margin targets - either opens or closes exit doors. Founders who start thinking about exits early make better decisions, full stop.

A strategic buyer acquiring a beverage brand is asking: "Does this fill a white space in our portfolio? Can we scale it through our distribution network? Does the brand positioning hold up at volume?" If you haven't been building toward answering those questions, you will not command a premium.

## Know the Buyer Universe Before You Need It

There are three types of acquirers for CPG brands, and they value your business very differently.

**Strategic buyers** - the Coca-Colas, Unilevers, General Mills, Nestles of the world, plus the emerging mid-tier strategics like Molson Coors Beverage Company's venture arm or Keurig Dr Pepper. They pay for category fit, brand equity, and distribution that complements their existing network. They will often pay 3-5x revenue for the right brand in a hot category. They are not buying your EBITDA - they're buying your brand and your channel penetration, then they will layer on their cost structure.

**Financial buyers** - private equity firms. They're buying cash flow and growth trajectory. They will put leverage on the business and expect to sell it again in 4-7 years. They are EBITDA-focused, typically paying 8-12x EBITDA for scaled businesses. If you're not yet profitable or are pre-profitability, most PE firms won't be your buyer - though some growth equity firms will bridge that gap.

**Brand aggregators and roll-up platforms** - these have proliferated over the last decade. They're buying proven brands to fold into a portfolio and wring out cost synergies. They pay less than strategics but move faster and are more flexible on deal structure.

Most founder exits in CPG happen to strategics. If that's your likely outcome, build your brand so it looks like something a strategic would acquire - not just a great small business.

## The Three Things Strategics Actually Care About

When Coca-Cola looks at an acquisition, they're running a checklist. I know because I've been on both the pitch side and the conversation side. Here's what's on that checklist:

**1. Is the brand in a category they want to win?**
Functional beverages, plant-based, health and wellness, reduced sugar - these are categories where strategics have consistently paid premiums. If your brand is in a declining or flat category, your exit options narrow dramatically. Category selection is an exit decision, even if it doesn't feel like one when you're starting out.

**2. Can the brand survive the mother ship?**
A lot of acquired brands die after acquisition. The acquirer knows this. They want proof that your brand has legs beyond your founder hustle - repeat purchase rates, strong retailer relationships, consumer loyalty that isn't personality-dependent. At Suja, we built a brand with real consumer pull. That mattered to Coca-Cola.

**3. Is the team transferable?**
In most strategic acquisitions, the founder exits within 12-24 months. The acquirer knows this going in. What they're asking is: is there a team that can run this without you? If the answer is no, that's a risk discount on your valuation.

## Gross Margin Is the Exit Multiplier

I've written separately about why 40% gross margin is the floor, not the ceiling. But in exit planning, this becomes even more critical.

Strategics have sophisticated finance teams running acquisition models. A brand at 35% gross margin gets a very different model outcome than a brand at 55% gross margin - and that difference compounds into purchase price.

If you are running at sub-40% gross margins going into a sale process, you have a few options: delay the exit and fix the margin, accept a lower multiple, or find a buyer who believes they can fix it post-acquisition (and negotiate a price that reflects that risk). None of those are great. Fix your margins before you go to market.

Target: 50%+ gross margin before you run a formal sale process. Yes, that is achievable in most CPG categories. It requires discipline on pricing, co-man negotiation, and cost of goods management.

## Create Competitive Tension in Your Process

The single biggest mistake I see founders make in exit negotiations: going exclusive too early.

Here's the dynamic. A buyer - whether strategic or financial - has a strong incentive to get you exclusive as fast as possible. Once you're exclusive, their leverage goes up and yours goes down. Every week you spend in exclusive due diligence is a week where you're not talking to other buyers, your team is distracted, and you're burning time and energy on a deal that might not close.

Run a real process. Even if you have an obvious lead buyer, get 2-3 other parties in the room. A signed letter of intent from a second buyer is worth more than any verbal indication from your first choice.

The moment a strategic knows another party is interested, the price gets better. It almost always gets better. Sometimes dramatically better.

I've seen deals where the initial offer was $40M and the final price was $75M. What changed? The founder stopped acting grateful and started acting like they had options. Because they did.

## Timing Your Exit: The Revenue and Velocity Window

Strategics acquire brands at the inflection point - after you've proven the concept but before you've captured the full market. They're buying the growth curve, not the plateau.

The ideal window for most strategic acquisitions in CPG: $20M-$100M in annual revenue, growing 30%+ year over year, with strong distribution velocity (sales per point of distribution trending up, not flat or declining).

Below $10M, most strategics won't look at you - the deal isn't big enough to move the needle and the integration cost is the same regardless. Above $150M with slowing growth, you're no longer the high-growth asset - you're more of a mature brand that gets valued differently.

Know where you are on that curve. If you're at $15M and growing fast, you might wait 12-18 months to hit the sweet spot. If you're at $60M and growth is starting to slow, you may want to go sooner rather than later.

## Get Your House in Order: 18 Months Out

A lot of founders start cleaning up their business when a buyer shows interest. That's too late. Due diligence will surface everything, and surprises in due diligence kill deals or crater price.

Eighteen months before you plan to go to market, do a self-audit:

- **Financial statements**: Get them audited or at minimum reviewed by a reputable CPA firm. Buyers do not trust founder-prepared financials.
- **Cap table**: Clean it up. Any weird side letters, convertible notes with unusual terms, or equity promises you made verbally need to be formalized or resolved. A messy cap table kills deals.
- **Customer concentration**: If one retailer is 50%+ of your revenue, that's a risk flag. Diversify your distribution.
- **IP**: Trademarks registered in the U.S. and key international markets. Patent protection where relevant. All IP owned by the company, not by you personally.
- **Key contracts**: Co-man agreements, supplier relationships, broker agreements - are these transferable? Do they have change-of-control provisions that could complicate a deal?

Fix these things before a buyer finds them. Every issue a buyer surfaces in due diligence becomes a negotiating chip against you.

## Choosing Your Advisor

You will need an investment banker or M&A advisor for a transaction above $20M. The question is which one.

Use a firm that knows CPG. This is not the moment to hire your cousin who does real estate deals. The buyer's team will have done dozens of CPG acquisitions. You need someone on your side who has been in those rooms before.

For most emerging CPG brands, the right advisor is a boutique with a CPG specialty - firms like Cascadia Capital, Lazard's consumer group, Houlihan Lokey's consumer practice, or one of several regional boutiques with strong food and beverage track records. They know which buyers are active, which ones are serious, and how to structure a process that creates competitive tension.

Their fee is worth it. A good banker will pay for themselves many times over in the final negotiated price.

## The Emotional Reality

I'll be direct: exiting a company you built is not easy, even when it's the right move. There's grief in it. The brand you built from a kitchen or a co-man facility becomes something that belongs to someone else. Your team's future is in other hands.

At Suja, that was real for me. We built something that mattered - a juice brand that was genuinely changing how people consumed fruits and vegetables. Handing that to Coca-Cola meant trusting that the brand would survive the transition.

The way I've come to think about it: a great exit is proof that you built something real. The market is paying you fair value because what you built has value. That's worth celebrating, even if it's also bittersweet.

Do the work to get there. Build the brand right. Run a real process. Know your number and know your walk-away. Then make the call that's right for your company, your investors, and your family.

That's the exit playbook.
