The call came in from a Costco buyer at our San Diego office. He wanted to run a head-to-head test: Suja's wellness shot against a competitor's product, side by side in clubs across the Pacific Northwest. And oh, by the way... could we also develop a plant-based milk? Costco wanted to test that too.

The production team's reaction was not enthusiasm. We had never made plant-based milk. It was outside everything we did well. Operationally messy, low in our core capabilities, a distraction we didn't need.

We did it anyway.

The milk struggled. The wellness shot sold about $2,000 per club per week.

That experience crystallized everything I know about Costco. It is simultaneously the most rewarding and most unforgiving channel in CPG. Forgiving because the volume and velocity, when you get it right, are unlike anything else in the industry. Unforgiving because Costco does not have time for products that aren't performing. You perform or you don't. And you find out fast.

Here's what I've learned about winning at Costco, and what every founder needs to understand before they get the call.

## Costco Is Not a Grocery Store

I know. Obvious, right?

But the number of brands I've seen approach Costco like they'd approach a regional grocery chain... it's remarkable. Same strategy. Same sell sheet. Same assumptions.

Here's the reality check. A mid-tier grocery account expects your product to move maybe $15 per store per week. That's a decent base. At that velocity, you keep the shelf, maybe you grow.

Costco expects $1,000 per club per week. Minimum.

That's not a typo. Sixty-seven times more volume. Let that number sit with you for a second.

The math makes sense when you understand what a Costco club actually is. The average club does over $200 million in annual sales. Members are committed buyers. They have a membership card. They show up specifically to purchase. The purchase intent is completely different from someone wandering down a grocery aisle.

If your product is right for that environment... Costco is one of the most powerful velocity engines in all of retail. If it's not right, you'll know in 13 weeks.

## The 13-Week Rotation: Understanding the Treasure Hunt

Most CPG founders, when they first hear about Costco's rotation model, get nervous. Thirteen weeks feels short. What if you don't have enough velocity data in the first few weeks? What if there's a supply hiccup?

I get it. But here's the thing most founders miss: the rotation model is also one of the best test-and-learn environments in CPG. Costco has deliberately created a "treasure hunt" dynamic in their stores. Members shop knowing that what's there today might not be there next month. That creates urgency. Urgency drives purchase. And for a brand that performs... it creates serious velocity.

At Suja, we developed a real collaborative approach with our Costco buyers. Multiple innovation offers in 10-week rotations, each one tracked simply: an X for a rotation that underperformed, a star for one that worked.

Failed rotation? X. That's okay. Next offer.

Successful rotation? Star. Build on it.

What we didn't do was spend 3 extra months perfecting each offer before bringing it to market. We kept the cadence. We stayed in genuine dialogue with the buyer. And most importantly... we were the first to tell them when something wasn't performing. Not waiting for the data conversation. Not being defensive. Just honest: "This one's not working. Here's what we're bringing next."

That built trust. And trust with a Costco buyer is worth a lot more than one rotation's worth of data.

There's something worth saying about how Costco is structured, too. Costco operates in regional divisions, and those regional divisions individually rival what would be national accounts at most other retailers. Getting into the Pacific Northwest division is real volume. Getting into the Northeast is real volume. Build your success story one region at a time before trying to go national inside their system. A great regional track record is the proof point that opens the next conversation.

## Sequencing Matters: When to Go After Costco

Here's a framework I use when coaching founders on retail expansion strategy.

Build your presence at Whole Foods and Target first. Then go after Costco. Then Kroger if you haven't already. And Walmart should come last.

Costco buyers want proof. Not just that your product tastes good or that your brand story is compelling. They want evidence that consumers already know and trust the brand. They're running a treasure hunt, but they're not looking for total unknowns. They're looking for products that consumers will recognize and immediately reach for.

Whole Foods builds credibility with better-for-you consumers. Target proves mainstream scalability. Those proof points change the Costco conversation entirely.

(The Walmart sequencing deserves its own post, but the short version: Walmart's EDLP model creates pricing tension across channels that can undermine your brand equity if you get in too early. Build the brand first. Then negotiate with Walmart from a position of strength.)

Don't confuse distribution gains with velocity gains. I say this constantly and it applies nowhere more critically than Costco. If your door count grew from 200 stores to 600 stores but your sales per point of distribution are flat or declining... that's not a strong velocity story. That's a red flag to a sophisticated buyer. Costco buyers are not sophisticated in the buzzword sense. They're sophisticated in the sense that they see a lot of data, they've seen a lot of brands, and they can spot the difference between a brand that's growing because the product is working and a brand that's growing because the founder is good at selling meetings.

## The Gross Margin Test You Cannot Skip

This is where I see founders make decisions that feel like wins but are quietly destroying them financially.

Costco does not have room for low-margin products. Not really.

At Suja, kombucha was running at roughly 12% gross margin because we were outsourcing it. Wellness shots, produced internally, were generating margins closer to 60%. When the Costco velocity data started rolling in... it wasn't even close. The shots were the business.

Swapping kombucha for wellness shots moved our company-wide gross margin from around 32% to around 40%. That's not a minor improvement. That's a transformation in the economics of the entire company.

"Gross margin determines destiny." I say it a lot. In the context of Costco, it's not abstract philosophy. The velocity at Costco is so high that a low-margin product can look like a success on the top line while absolutely wrecking you financially.

CPG is a "penny profit" business. The pennies matter. And at Costco's volume, those pennies compound faster than anywhere else in retail.

Run the math before you take the meeting. What's your landed cost? What's Costco's required margin? What does your trade spend look like at this volume? What does your cost structure look like at 10,000 units a week per club? If you can't answer those questions clearly... do the work first.

## What Costco Buyers Actually Want From You

Here's what doesn't move the needle with a Costco buyer: a slick brand story, a beautiful sell sheet, a massive Instagram following.

Here's what does: velocity data they can believe in.

Costco buyers are thinking about how fast your product moves off a pallet, not off a shelf. The format matters. The price point matters. The visual impact on a pallet display matters. A product that's beautiful at Whole Foods in a 12-oz bottle might need to be completely rethought for a Costco 32-oz value pack. These are not the same product. Don't treat them like they are.

Bring velocity data you're genuinely proud of. Know your sales per point of distribution. Know your category ranking. Know how your turns compare to the category median. If you're at or above the 50th percentile and trending up... you have a story. Tell it clearly and simply.

And when the buyer challenges you with an "out there" idea, like developing a product category you've never worked in before... hear them out. Sometimes the plant milk fails and the wellness shot takes off. You don't always know which is which until you run it. What you can control is whether you show up honest, stay in the conversation, and keep the relationship intact through both the Xs and the stars.

## The Bottom Line

Costco can be genuinely transformative. Wellness shots doing $2,000 per week per club, across hundreds of locations, became one of the most important growth engines Suja ever had. That's not a small thing. We captured over 30% market share within a year of entering as the third player behind VIVE and KOR.

But Costco is not a shortcut. It's a high-stakes, high-reward relationship that rewards brands that arrive prepared, play it straight, and earn trust over time.

Do the margin math. Build your velocity story before you walk in the door. Get into the right sequence. And when the buyer marks your first rotation with an X... be the first to call them, own it, and show up with a star.

That's the game. And it's worth playing.
