Products don't build companies. People do. And the wrong people - or the wrong relationships with the right people - will sink your brand faster than any competitor. Here are the five people and focus fatal flaws I've seen across 8 companies and dozens of investments.

## 1. Giving Away Equity Too Early

This one haunts founders for years. In the early days, when cash is tight and excitement is high, it's tempting to compensate friends, family, and early helpers with equity. "Help me with our website and I'll give you 2%." "Come on as an advisor and take 1%." Those casual promises add up fast.

I've seen founders who gave away 15-20% of their company before they ever raised a dime - to people who contributed a few weekends of work. Worse, those equity holders now have a say in your cap table, and they can create complications in every future fundraising round.

**The fix:** Never give equity casually. If someone is contributing real value, put them on a vesting schedule with a cliff. If they want equity for a few hours of work, pay them cash or don't work with them. Equity is the most expensive currency you have.

## 2. Failed Partnerships and Bad Key Hires

A co-founder breakup in year one is like a divorce - except the assets are your company. Early falling outs between partners create toxic environments that infect your entire team. And a bad key hire - a VP of Sales who can't sell, a head of ops who can't operate - can cost you 6-12 months of progress.

The damage isn't just the direct cost. It's the opportunity cost. While you're dealing with the fallout, your competitors are moving. Retailers lose confidence. Investors get nervous.

**The fix:** Move quickly when it's not working. Don't let a bad partnership or hire linger hoping it'll get better. It won't. Have clear operating agreements from day one that spell out roles, decision-making authority, and exit terms. And for key hires, always do a 90-day trial period with clear performance expectations.

## 3. Taking Bad Advice

The CPG world is full of people who will give you advice. Most of them have never actually built a brand. They read about it, they consulted on it, they invested in one once. But they've never been in the arena.

The danger of bad advice is that it sounds reasonable. "You should be in 5,000 doors by year two." "You need to spend 30% of revenue on marketing." These might be true for a mature brand but could be fatal for a startup. And when something feels off, too many founders ignore their gut because the advisor has a fancy title.

**The fix:** Always cross-check advice with a trusted, unbiased third party who has actually built and scaled CPG brands. If someone's advice doesn't pass the gut check, dig deeper before acting on it. Credentials don't equal competence.

## 4. Mixing Family and Friends with Business

Family and friends can be incredible business partners - or they can destroy both the business and the relationship. The difference comes down to one thing: clear expectations.

I've seen brothers stop speaking over inventory decisions. Best friends become enemies over equity splits. Parents fund a business and then expect to control it. The problem is never the relationship itself. The problem is that nobody sat down and had the hard conversation about roles, compensation, decision-making, and what happens if it doesn't work out.

**The fix:** Treat family and friends exactly like you'd treat any business partner. Written agreements. Clear roles. Defined compensation. Exit terms. Having the hard conversation upfront is uncomfortable for 30 minutes. Not having it can be toxic for years.

## 5. Losing Focus After Early Success

This is the most insidious fatal flaw because it comes from a good place. You land Whole Foods. You get a feature in Forbes. Velocity is up. And suddenly you're distracted. You're doing speaking engagements, exploring new product lines, having conversations with potential acquirers - all while the core business needs your full attention.

The practices that got you to this point - hustling at store demos, managing every retailer relationship personally, watching cash flow daily - those don't stop mattering because you had a good quarter. I've watched founders coast after early wins and lose everything they built.

**The fix:** Stay paranoid. Stay hungry. The habits that got you here are the habits that keep you here. Block time every week for the fundamentals. Don't let the exciting stuff crowd out the essential stuff.
